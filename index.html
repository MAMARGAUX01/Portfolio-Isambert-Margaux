UX Case Study Concept project Solo

Interior Design Project Management Platform

My RoleUX Researcher & Designer
ToolsFigma · Notion
TypeConcept / Portfolio project
01 Context

The landscape

Interior architecture projects involve many actors: designers, clients, contractors, suppliers and manufacturers. Projects can last several months and require constant coordination between all stakeholders.

During my work in an interior design studio, I observed that most project management still happens through a mix of phone calls, emails, spreadsheets and scattered documents. Designers spend a significant amount of time following up with suppliers, answering client questions and checking project status manually.

This creates delays in communication, lost information and unnecessary stress for both designers and clients.

This project explores how a digital platform could centralise project communication and information, giving both the studio and the client a clearer view of the project at every stage.

02 Problem Statement

The core tension

Interior designers need to coordinate complex projects involving many stakeholders, but the tools they currently use are fragmented and not adapted to their workflow.

As a result:

Designers spend a large portion of their time chasing information instead of designing

Clients lack visibility on project progress and often feel uncertain during long projects

Information can be lost between emails, calls and documents

"The challenge was to design a simple and transparent project management platform that improves communication between designers and clients while reducing administrative workload for the studio."

03 Research & Discovery

Understanding the problem space

Before designing the interface, I focused on understanding how interior designers currently manage their projects and where the friction occurs. Research was based on three main sources.

👁
Studio observation
Working alongside interior architects, I observed recurring situations: designers frequently switching between phone calls, emails and spreadsheets; clients requesting updates because they lacked visibility on project progress; information about suppliers or deliveries being difficult to track. These observations highlighted how fragmented the current workflow is.
🗣
Informal interviews
Conversations with designers about daily coordination challenges and current tools. Key topics: communication with clients, supplier and delivery tracking, project organisation methods, pain points in the workflow.
🔍
Competitive analysis
Analysed existing tools: Asana, Monday, Notion, Houzz Pro, Studio Designer. While powerful, most of these tools are either too generic or designed for construction or software teams, not for interior design studios.
[ Research documentation — assets/cs-research.jpg ]

Studio observation and interview notes highlighted how fragmented the current workflow is.

04 Key Insights

What the research revealed

Four main insights emerged from research and observation:

01

Communication with clients consumes a large amount of time. Designers frequently answer similar questions from clients about project status, timelines or approvals. Many of these interactions could be simplified if clients had direct access to project updates.

02

Information is scattered across multiple tools. Budgets, supplier information, design references and schedules often live in different places. This fragmentation increases the risk of miscommunication and delays.

03

Clients want reassurance during long projects. Interior design projects often involve significant financial investment. Clients want to feel involved and informed throughout the process. Transparency plays a key role in building trust and confidence.

04

Designers want to focus on creativity. Administrative coordination takes time away from the creative work designers value most. Any solution should therefore reduce administrative overhead rather than add more complexity.

05 Design Objectives

Design goals

Based on these insights, I defined three main objectives for the platform:

Create a shared project space. Clients and designers should be able to access the same information in real time, reducing the need for repeated updates.

Centralise project information. All key elements — project progress, moodboards, documents and timelines — should be accessible in one place.

Improve transparency throughout the project. Clear visibility on project milestones helps reassure clients and reduces uncertainty during long projects.

06 Wireframes & Prototyping

From idea to interface

The design process started with simple sketches exploring how information could be structured. I then created mid-fidelity wireframes in Figma and built an interactive prototype to test the main flows.

The prototype focused on three key moments in the client journey:

07 Solution

The proposed platform

The concept platform is structured around three main components:

Appointment system

A simple scheduling flow allows clients to book their first consultation quickly without needing to contact the studio manually. This replaces the typical back-and-forth email scheduling.

Project dashboard

Each client receives a personalised project page displaying: project overview, design moodboards, current project phase, and important milestones. Clients stay informed without needing to request updates.

Project timeline

A visual timeline shows the different stages of the project, allowing clients to understand where they are in the process. This improves transparency and reduces uncertainty.

08 Testing & Evaluation

Prototype feedback

The prototype was informally reviewed by designers familiar with interior design workflows. Feedback focused on:

Clarity of the project dashboard and ease of reading project status at a glance

Usefulness of the timeline for client communication during long projects

Simplicity of the booking flow and reduction of scheduling friction

Overall reactions suggested that the platform could help simplify communication between designers and clients. If developed further, the product could be evaluated using metrics such as:

↓
Reduction in client update requests to the studio
↓
Time spent on administrative coordination per project
↑
Client satisfaction during projects and adoption rate among designers
09 Reflection & Learnings

What I learned

This project helped me understand how deeply work practices influence product design. Interior designers do not only need functional tools — they also care about how those tools reflect their professional image when interacting with clients.

Transparency and simplicity emerged as key design principles for this context.

"Next steps would include: testing the prototype with more interior designers, exploring mobile features for on-site updates, designing supplier management features, and developing higher fidelity UI and design system."

Starting point

This project came directly from observation. Working inside a studio gave me access to the real friction — not a hypothetical version of it.

Research note

Studio observation was the most revealing method. Seeing the workflow in real time — the switching between tools, the interruptions — was more useful than asking about it in interviews.

Design decision

I centred the platform on the client experience rather than the designer's back-end — because client anxiety was the primary source of unnecessary designer workload.

Next step

The supplier management layer is the most complex remaining problem. It's where I'd focus next — linking deliveries directly to the client-facing timeline.

Brand Strategy UX Research Product Concept Solo

YUHWA — K-Beauty Brand Concept

My RoleUX Research · Brand Strategy · Product Concept
Duration6 weeks
ToolsCanva · Adobe Creative Suite · Notion
TypeAcademic / Concept project
01 Context

The landscape

K-beauty has become one of the most influential segments in the global skincare industry. Western consumers increasingly seek Korean skincare products for their innovation, layered routines and ingredient philosophy.

However, many brands entering Western markets face a challenge: balancing cultural authenticity with scientific credibility. Some brands emphasise heritage and traditional ingredients, while others focus on dermatological performance.

This project explored how a new skincare brand could position itself within this landscape while addressing the expectations of Western premium skincare consumers.

02 Problem Statement

The core tension

Many K-beauty brands entering Western markets struggle to communicate both cultural authenticity and scientific credibility at the same time.

Some brands focus heavily on tradition and storytelling but lack perceived scientific authority. Others highlight clinical performance but lose the cultural identity that originally differentiates Korean skincare.

"The challenge was to design a brand concept that bridges these two dimensions, combining Korean ingredient heritage with credible scientific validation."

03 Research & Discovery

Understanding the market

The research phase focused on identifying positioning gaps within the premium K-beauty market.

🔍
Competitive analysis
Analysed four established K-beauty brands operating internationally: Sulwhasoo, SK-II, Dr. Jart+, Erborian. Each was mapped across two key positioning axes: cultural authenticity and scientific credibility.
🗺
Market positioning map
The analysis revealed that most brands tend to emphasise one dimension over the other, highlighting a potential opportunity space for a brand combining traditional Korean skincare philosophy with clinically validated formulas.
04 Key Insights

What the research revealed

Three insights guided the development of the brand concept:

01

Western consumers seek authenticity. Consumers interested in K-beauty are drawn to the cultural heritage behind the products. Traditional ingredients and rituals are seen as part of the brand's value.

02

Scientific credibility builds trust. Premium skincare buyers want reassurance that products are effective and safe. Clinical validation and dermatological research play a key role in building that trust.

03

Brand identity shapes perceived product quality. In the premium skincare market, visual identity and storytelling strongly influence how consumers perceive the product. A coherent brand universe is therefore essential.

05 Design Objectives

Strategic goals

Create a positioning that combines Korean skincare heritage with scientific credibility

Develop a minimal and premium visual identity reflecting calmness and balance

Design a product line structured around a clear skincare ritual

06 Concept Development

YUHWA (유화)

The brand concept developed for this project is YUHWA, inspired by the Korean word meaning softness and harmony. The concept centres around the idea of balance between tradition and modern science.

Product philosophy

The brand narrative combines hanbang ingredients traditionally used in Korean herbal medicine with modern dermatological research validating ingredient efficacy — communicating both cultural depth and scientific credibility.

Product architecture

The initial concept includes a three-product ritual designed to simplify the skincare routine while maintaining the layered philosophy typical of Korean skincare. The range was intentionally limited to emphasise clarity and product quality.

Visual identity

The visual direction reflects harmony and calm: muted natural colour palette, editorial-inspired typography, minimal packaging design, bilingual brand language. The goal was to create a brand identity that feels refined, calm and culturally grounded.

07 Solution

Brand ecosystem

The final concept includes: brand name and positioning, visual identity direction, product architecture, and a narrative connecting heritage and science.

Rather than focusing purely on aesthetics, the project explored how strategy, storytelling and product logic work together to create a coherent brand.

[ Brand identity visuals — assets/yuhwa-identity.jpg ]

Visual identity direction — muted palette, editorial typography, bilingual brand language.

08 Reflection & Learnings

What I learned

This project showed how branding is closely linked to user perception and trust. Consumers do not only evaluate product performance — they also interpret signals from brand storytelling, design and cultural references.

One of the main challenges was translating Korean cultural elements without simplifying them for a Western audience. This reinforced the importance of respectful research and thoughtful positioning when designing cross-cultural products.

The opportunity

The positioning map was the most clarifying tool — seeing where every major brand sat made the gap immediately visible.

Key tension

Balancing cultural authenticity with Western legibility without flattening the cultural nuance was the hardest design challenge throughout.

What I'd do next

I'd test the brand concept with both Korean and Western consumers to see whether the cultural signals land differently across audiences.

Botani'Kube — Smart Indoor Garden

My RoleMarket Research · Product Strategy
Team3 people
Duration6 weeks
ToolsGoogle Forms · Canva · Adobe Creative Suite · Notion
01 Context

The landscape

Urban lifestyles increasingly limit access to outdoor space and natural environments. At the same time, awareness of the relationship between indoor environments and wellbeing has grown significantly.

Indoor gardening products have emerged as a way for city residents to reconnect with plants, even in small apartments. Several companies already offer smart indoor gardens, combining automated lighting, watering and seed pods.

However, many consumers remain hesitant to adopt these products.

02 Problem Statement

The plant anxiety paradox

Research suggested a contradiction in consumer behaviour. Many people express interest in having plants at home, yet a large proportion avoid buying them.

"A key reason is fear of not being able to keep them alive. The challenge was therefore not simply to design a gardening product, but to explore how a product could reduce the anxiety associated with plant care."

03 Research & Discovery

Market analysis

The team analysed several existing smart indoor gardening products and their business models.

🔍
Competitive analysis
Analysed Véritable, Prêt à Pousser, and Click & Grow — products offering automated growing systems using seed capsules and integrated lighting.
💡
Business model analysis
One common element among competitors: proprietary seed capsules requiring users to buy refills from the same brand. This creates recurring revenue but limits flexibility and can generate user frustration.

"This analysis revealed a potential opportunity: a smart indoor garden that offers automation while maintaining greater user autonomy."

04 Key Insights

What the research revealed

Three insights shaped the final concept:

01

Plant care anxiety is a major barrier. Many potential users worry about forgetting to water plants or choosing the wrong conditions. Automation can help reduce this stress.

02

Consumers dislike restrictive ecosystems. Closed systems based on proprietary capsules can create frustration and reduce perceived freedom. Users prefer products that allow flexibility.

03

Sustainability matters. Many consumers interested in indoor gardening are also sensitive to environmental concerns. Reducing waste and encouraging responsible growing practices can therefore be a strong differentiator.

05 Design Objectives

Product goals

Reduce anxiety around plant care through automation

Allow users to grow plants using standard seeds rather than proprietary capsules

Promote sustainable growing through a low-waste system

06 Solution

Botani'Kube

Botani'Kube is a concept for a smart indoor garden designed for urban apartments. The product integrates several automated features to simplify plant care.

Automated features

Automatic irrigation system, integrated LED lighting adapted to plant growth, and smart sensors monitoring humidity and plant conditions — removing the need for daily maintenance.

Open seed ecosystem

Unlike many competitors, the concept allows users to grow plants using standard seeds rather than proprietary capsules. This gives users more freedom and reduces dependency on a specific brand ecosystem.

Sustainability focus

The concept includes a small integrated compost module designed to reuse organic kitchen waste, supporting a more circular and sustainable growing approach.

07 Business Model

Go-to-market strategy

The product would be sold as a one-time purchase within a mid-range price bracket. Optional seed kits could be offered for convenience, but the system would remain compatible with standard seeds.

Direct-to-consumer e-commerce as primary channel

Eco-focused concept stores for brand visibility and tactile discovery

Home lifestyle retailers for broader market reach

08 Reflection & Learnings

What I learned

One of the most important insights from this project was the role of problem framing. At first, the team approached the project as a convenience product for growing plants indoors.

However, research revealed that the real barrier was not convenience but anxiety about plant care. Reframing the problem around emotional barriers helped guide more relevant design decisions.

"This project reinforced the importance of deeply understanding user motivations before designing solutions."

Problem framing

We almost designed around the wrong problem (convenience) until research revealed anxiety as the core blocker. The reframe changed everything.

Key differentiator

The open seed standard was both a functional and strategic decision — it directly addressed the frustration we found with competitor lock-in.

What I'd do next

I'd test whether the anxiety framing resonates in user interviews, and validate the price point against willingness-to-pay for the automation features.
