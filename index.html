UX Case Study B2B SaaS Concept project Solo

Interior Design Project Management Platform

My RoleUX Researcher & Designer
Duration8 weeks
ToolsFigma · Maze · Miro · Notion
TypeConcept / Portfolio project
01 Context

The landscape

Luxury interior design studios manage complex, multi-month projects involving architects, craftspeople, suppliers, and exacting clients. Yet nearly all of them coordinate through spreadsheets, email chains, and generic tools like Trello — built for software teams, not design studios.

The industry has almost no dedicated digital infrastructure. That gap is the starting point.

02 Problem Statement

The core tension

"Interior designers spend up to 30% of project time on administrative coordination rather than design work — yet no existing PM tool speaks their language or reflects the non-linear nature of their process."

The challenge wasn't simply to build a project management tool. It was to design one that felt native to the craft-led, relationship-heavy, aesthetics-driven context of luxury interior design — not borrowed from construction or software development.

03 Research & Discovery

Getting close to the problem

Before sketching anything, I spent three weeks inside the problem space — understanding not just what designers needed, but how they thought about their work, what language they used, and what they were proud of.

🗣
5 qualitative interviews
Interior designers and studio PMs, 25–60 min each. Focus: daily coordination habits, current tools, pain points, workarounds.
🔍
Competitive audit (6 tools)
Asana, Monday.com, Houzz Pro, Studio Designer, Ivy, Notion — each mapped against interior design workflow needs.
👁
Contextual observation
2 days embedded in a studio. Observed how projects were tracked, suppliers managed, and clients updated in real time.
📋
Survey (n=18)
Validated interview findings at scale with members of interior design professional communities.
[ Affinity map / research board — assets/cs-research.jpg ]

Affinity mapping across 5 interviews revealed four recurring friction themes.

04 Key Insights

What the research revealed

Four findings shaped every subsequent design decision. They're ordered by impact on the final design direction:

01

Designers resist tools that "feel corporate." PM software is mentally associated with construction sites and IT departments — not their world. The tool's aesthetic is a functional requirement, not a nice-to-have.

02

Client communication is the biggest time sink. Chasing approvals, formatting status updates, handling informal message chains — this is where project hours disappear.

03

Budget tracking and supplier timelines live in 3–5 disconnected tools. Reconciling them takes 30–90 minutes per project per week.

04

When a designer opens a new tool in front of a client and it looks beautiful, trust in their professionalism rises. Aesthetic credibility is a usability factor — not vanity.

05 Design Objectives

What I set out to solve

Based on the research, I defined three primary design goals — and one unstated constraint that shaped all of them:

Reduce client communication overhead by providing a shared, always-current project view that eliminates the need for weekly status emails.

Centralise supplier timelines and budget tracking in one place — linked to specific projects, visible at a glance, one source of truth.

Design an interface that feels at home in a design studio — not a construction firm. Visual quality is a product requirement from day one.

The unstated constraint: every design decision had to be defensible to a skeptical, aesthetics-first user. If it looked like enterprise software, it was wrong — regardless of how well it worked.

06 Wireframes & Prototyping

From sketches to mid-fidelity

I started with paper sketches to explore structure without committing to visual decisions. Low-fidelity wireframes followed, then a mid-fidelity interactive Figma prototype tested with 3 interior designers in two rounds of sessions.

07 Solution

What I designed

A platform structured around four modules, each addressing one of the core friction points identified in research:

Project Timeline

Visual Gantt structured around design phases (concept, sourcing, installation) — not generic task categories. Phases are visual and spatial, not textual.

Client Portal

Studio-branded client view with milestone visibility, mood board check-ins, and structured approval flows. Replaces informal message chains entirely.

Supplier Hub

Centralised directory with delivery status, order tracking, and per-project budget integration. One source of truth for procurement.

Studio Dashboard

Principal-level view: all active projects, milestone health, pending approvals, and budget alerts on one screen.

08 Testing & Success Metrics

How I'd measure success

I ran two rounds of usability testing with the mid-fidelity prototype. For a concept project, I also defined the metrics I would use to evaluate real-world success:

4d→0
Client approval cycle time — from avg. 4 days to same-day
NPS
Studio-user Net Promoter Score after 30-day pilot
100%
Whole-studio adoption — not just the principal designer

Task completion rate on core flows: create project, send client update, log supplier status.

Time-on-task reduction vs. current workflow — baselined from contextual observation sessions.

09 Reflection & Learnings

What I took away

B2B design is as much about professional identity and trust as it is about functionality. The strongest signal from research: designers don't resist digital tools — they resist tools that feel alien to their practice.

Aesthetic credibility isn't cosmetic. In a context where the tool is opened in front of a €200k client, how it looks is part of how it works. That forced me to treat visual design as a primary requirement from day one, not a layer added at the end.

"If I were to continue: high-fidelity usability testing, a mobile notification layer for on-site updates, and a freemium entry point to lower the adoption barrier for solo designers."

Why this project

I wanted to practice end-to-end UX in a domain where the design problem is genuinely unsolved — most interior design studios are running on workarounds.

Research note

The contextual observation was the most revealing method. Seeing how decisions actually get made — in the moment, between tools — was far more useful than asking about them in interviews.

Design decision

I decided to show the client portal before the Gantt timeline in the product flow — client communication was the #1 pain point, so it had to feel like the core feature.

What I'd do differently

I'd recruit 2 additional participants for usability testing, specifically studio assistants rather than only principals — their workflow is where adoption either succeeds or dies.

YUHWA — K-Beauty Brand Concept

RoleBrand strategist & designer (solo)
ContextAcademic, Bachelor's Year 3
01 The Problem

The gap in the market

"Premium K-beauty consumers must choose between cultural authenticity and scientific rigour. The whitespace: a brand that credibly delivers both."

02 Research

Competitive analysis & gap identification

Benchmarked Sulwhasoo, SK-II, Dr. Jart+, and Erborian across a cultural authenticity / scientific rigour axis. Identified an underserved whitespace: anchoring hanbang fermentation in peer-reviewed dermatology research for a Western premium audience.

03 Solution

YUHWA (유화) — brand architecture

Name from the Korean word for softness and harmony

3-product signature line structuring a complete ritual narrative

Muted palette, editorial calm, bilingual typographic identity

Core tension held in every touchpoint: "Ancient Korean fermentation, clinically validated."

04 Reflection

What I learned

A strong brand is the coherence between story, product logic, and lived experience. The real challenge was translating cultural nuance into strategic positioning without flattening it into a single Western-legible narrative.

Context

This was my first full brand project from scratch. I used competitive mapping as my primary analytical tool before moving to positioning.

Retail ExperienceCustomer ResearchExperience Strategy

Maison Nicolas — In-Store Experience Redesign

RoleResearch lead & strategist
Team4 people
Budget delivered€19,500 / €50k brief
01 The Problem

The brand/experience disconnect

"Maison Nicolas had rebranded visually, but in-store the experience still felt like a supermarket wine aisle. The repositioning wasn't landing at the point of sale."

02 Research

Field audit & benchmarking

Two-store audit in Rennes, benchmarking of 8 competitors. Four critical friction points: harsh lighting, exposed stock, buried premium formats, zero degustation space.

03 Solution

3-axis repositioning strategy

Atmosphere: ambient lighting redesign, curated sound environment, reorganised merchandising hierarchy

Education: monthly editorial leaflet, food-and-wine pairing booklet, in-store degustation counter

Loyalty: dematerialised loyalty card + exclusive tasting events for registered customers

04 Reflection

What I learned

Brand transformation requires consistency at every touchpoint. A logo change achieves nothing if the physical environment contradicts it. This project directly shaped my interest in end-to-end experience design as a discipline — not just screen-based UX.

Key decision

We prioritised atmosphere over loyalty — because no loyalty mechanic will work if a customer doesn't feel they belong in the space.

Brand IdentityUser ResearchInclusive Design

Prisma — Inclusive Eyewear Brand

RoleBrand strategist & researcher (solo)
ContextAcademic project
01 The Problem

Binary design in a non-binary world

"The eyewear industry defaults to binary gender segmentation. Young queer consumers want authentic representation — not performative marketing."

02 Research

Sociological persona & community insight

Developed a sociological persona ("Sami", 28, non-binary, creative sector). Core finding: this audience rejects brands that "target" them — they want co-creation and genuine integration, not rainbow-washing.

03 Solution + Crisis Exercise

Brand identity & crisis response

Non-gendered visual identity: Japanese-influenced typography, desaturated palette, artisan-first manufacturing story (Strasbourg). Then handled a simulated cultural appropriation crisis — response anchored in genuine accountability, not PR deflection.

04 Reflection

What I learned

Inclusive design is a strategic commitment, not a visual style choice. Brands serving underrepresented communities are held to a higher standard of consistency — because their audience has seen too many brands treat them as a trend.

Research note

Sociological personas are more useful here than behavioural ones — the key variable is how the audience relates to cultural identity, not just how they shop.

Product StrategyMarket ResearchBusiness Model

Botani'Kube — Smart Indoor Garden Startup

RoleMarket researcher & product strategist
Team4 people
ContextAcademic project
01 The Problem

The plant anxiety paradox

"80% of French adults say their indoor environment affects wellbeing. 60% have no plants — not from lack of interest, but from anxiety about keeping them alive."

02 Research

Competitive gap analysis

Benchmarked Véritable, Prêt à Pousser, Click & Grow. All three monetise through proprietary capsule subscriptions that create lock-in and user resentment. Gap: a fully autonomous smart garden with open-standard seeds and no subscription dependency.

03 Solution

Product & business model

Autonomous indoor garden (auto-irrigation, auto-lighting) for urban apartments

Open seed standard — no proprietary capsule lock-in

Integrated compost system — zero-waste positioning

Price: €120–250 one-time. Optional seed subscription (not mandatory)

Distribution: DTC e-commerce + eco concept stores

04 Reflection

What I learned

The real problem wasn't "how to grow plants indoors." It was "how to remove the anxiety of keeping them alive." That reframe changed the entire product rationale. Correctly defining the problem is, in practice, the most important design decision.

Problem framing

We almost designed around the wrong problem (convenience) until interviews revealed anxiety as the core blocker. The pivot changed everything.
