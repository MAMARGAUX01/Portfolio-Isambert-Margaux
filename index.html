01 — Context

The landscape

Luxury interior design studios manage complex, multi-month projects involving architects, craftspeople, suppliers, and exacting clients. Yet most rely on spreadsheets, email chains, and generic tools like Trello. The industry has almost no dedicated digital infrastructure.

02 — Problem Statement

The core tension

"Interior designers spend up to 30% of project time on administrative coordination rather than design work — yet no existing PM tool speaks their language or reflects the non-linear nature of their process."

The challenge was not simply to build a PM tool, but to design one that respected the craft-led, relationship-heavy, aesthetics-driven nature of luxury interior design work.

03 — Research & Observations

How I gathered insight

  • 5 qualitative interviews with interior designers and PMs (25–60 min each)
  • Competitive analysis of 6 PM tools: Asana, Monday.com, Houzz Pro, Studio Designer, Ivy, Notion
  • Contextual observation of a studio's daily coordination workflow over 2 days
  • Survey with 18 respondents from interior design professional communities
[ Affinity map / research board — assets/cs-research.jpg ]

04 — Key Insights

What I found

Insight 01

Designers resist tools that "feel corporate." They associate PM software with construction or IT — not their world.

Insight 02

Client communication is the biggest time sink: chasing approvals, sending updates, managing informal feedback loops.

Insight 03

Budget tracking and supplier timelines live in disconnected tools, creating constant reconciliation overhead.

Insight 04

Aesthetic credibility drives adoption: in this world, a beautiful tool is perceived as more trustworthy.

05 — Objective

Design goals

Three primary design goals:

  1. Reduce client communication overhead via a shared, transparent project view
  2. Centralise supplier timelines and budget tracking in one visual, studio-branded space
  3. Design an interface that feels at home in a design studio — not a construction firm

06 — Solution

What I designed

A platform built specifically for interior design studios, structured around four core modules:

Project Timeline

Visual Gantt-style view adapted to design phases (concept, sourcing, installation) — not generic task lists.

Client Portal

Studio-branded client-facing view with approval flows, mood board updates, and milestone visibility. Eliminates status emails.

Supplier Hub

Centralised supplier directory with delivery status, order tracking, and per-project budget integration.

Studio Dashboard

High-level view of all active projects, milestones, and pending approvals — for studio principals.

07 — Wireframes & Prototype

From sketches to mid-fi

Paper sketches → low-fidelity wireframes → mid-fidelity interactive Figma prototype, tested with 3 interior designers.

08 — What I Would Measure

Success indicators

  • Task completion rate on core flows (create project, send client update, log supplier)
  • Time-on-task reduction vs. current workflow, baselined from research interviews
  • Client approval cycle time — target: reduce from avg. 4 days to same-day
  • NPS from studio users after a 30-day pilot
  • Team adoption rate — not just the principal designer, but the whole studio

09 — Learnings & Next Steps

What I took away

B2B design is as much about professional identity and trust as functionality. The strongest research insight: designers don't resist digital tools — they resist tools that feel alien to their practice. Aesthetic credibility is a usability factor here, not a vanity one.

If taken further: high-fidelity usability testing, a mobile notification layer for on-site updates, and a freemium model to lower adoption barriers for solo designers.

YUHWA — K-Beauty Brand Concept

RoleBrand strategist & designer (solo)
ContextAcademic, Bachelor's Year 3

Problem

"Premium K-beauty consumers must choose between cultural authenticity and scientific rigour. The whitespace: a brand that credibly delivers both."

Research

Competitive analysis & gap identification

Benchmarked Sulwhasoo, SK-II, Dr. Jart+, and Erborian. Identified an underserved whitespace: anchoring hanbang fermentation in peer-reviewed dermatology research for a Western premium audience.

Solution

YUHWA (유화) — brand architecture

  • Name from the Korean word for softness and harmony
  • 3-product signature line structuring a complete ritual narrative
  • Muted palette, editorial calm, bilingual typographic identity
  • Core tension: "Ancient Korean fermentation, clinically validated."

Takeaway

A strong brand is the coherence between story, product logic, and lived experience. The real challenge was translating cultural nuance into strategic positioning without flattening it.

Maison Nicolas —
In-Store Experience Redesign

RoleResearch lead & experience strategist
Team4 people
ContextAcademic project

Problem

"Maison Nicolas had rebranded visually, but in-store the experience still felt like a supermarket wine aisle. The repositioning wasn't landing at the point of sale."

Research

Field audit & competitor benchmarking

Two-store audit in Rennes, benchmarking of 8 competitors. Four friction points: harsh lighting, exposed stock, buried premium formats, zero degustation space.

Solution

3-axis repositioning strategy

  • Atmosphere: ambient lighting, curated sound, reorganised merchandising
  • Education: monthly leaflet, food-and-wine pairing booklet, degustation counter
  • Loyalty: dematerialised loyalty card + exclusive tasting events

Budget: €19,500 against a brief of €50,000.

Takeaway

Brand transformation requires consistency at every touchpoint. A logo change achieves nothing if the environment contradicts it.

Prisma — Inclusive Eyewear Brand

RoleBrand strategist & researcher (solo)
ContextAcademic project

Problem

"The eyewear industry defaults to binary gender segmentation. Young queer consumers want authentic representation — not performative marketing."

Research

Sociological persona & community insight

Developed a sociological persona ("Sami", 28, non-binary, creative sector). Core finding: this audience rejects brands that "target" them — they want co-creation, not rainbow-washing.

Solution + Crisis Exercise

Brand identity & crisis response

Non-gendered visual identity: Japanese-influenced typography, desaturated palette, artisan-first story (Strasbourg). Then handled a simulated cultural appropriation crisis — response anchored in genuine accountability, not PR deflection.

Takeaway

Inclusive design is a strategic commitment, not a visual style choice. Design decisions earn trust over time.

Botani'Kube —
Smart Indoor Garden Startup

RoleMarket researcher & product strategist
Team4 people
ContextAcademic project

Problem

"80% of French adults say their indoor environment affects wellbeing. 60% have no plants — not from lack of interest, but from anxiety about keeping them alive."

Research

Competitive gap analysis

Benchmarked Véritable, Prêt à Pousser, Click & Grow. All three use proprietary capsule subscriptions creating lock-in and user resentment. Gap: an autonomous smart garden with open-standard seeds and no subscription dependency.

Solution

Product & business model

  • Autonomous indoor garden (auto-irrigation, auto-lighting) for urban apartments
  • Open seed standard — no proprietary lock-in
  • Integrated compost system — zero-waste positioning
  • Price: €120–250 one-time. Optional seed subscription
  • Distribution: DTC e-commerce + eco concept stores

Takeaway

The real problem wasn't "how to grow plants indoors" — it was "how to remove the anxiety of keeping them alive." Correctly defining the problem is the most important design decision.
